Corpus overview & genre taxonomy

Corpus overview

The corpus is derived from the OpenITI / Shamela library and comprises 2,069 Arabic texts spanning more than a millennium of writing (centuries 2–15 AH in the current release). Texts are grouped into six major genres (BIO, DEV, PHI, POE, RHE, THE) in order to compare how literary conventions shape the expression of love across time and across discourse traditions.

What a “text” means here

Each entry corresponds to a distinct work as represented in the corpus (not chapters or excerpts). Genre labels reflect the dominant discursive tradition of the work (e.g., poetic, devotional/mystical, adab/rhetorical), acknowledging that boundary cases and hybrid works exist.

    What each genre captures

    BIO — Biographical (tarājim). Biographical works include lives of scholars, poets, rulers, saints, or notable figures. Love appears through anecdotes, reputational narratives, moral exempla, and accounts of relationships (sometimes implicitly), offering a lens on how love is framed through social status, virtue, and memory.

    DEV — Devotional / Mystical (taʿabbudī / ṣūfī). Devotional and mystical texts foreground love as a spiritual practice: longing, devotion, purification, divine union, and moral self-work. This genre is key to capturing mystical love and the language of desire redirected toward the divine, often through rich metaphorical registers.

    PHI — Philosophical (falsafī). Philosophical texts include discussions of ethics, the soul, virtues, and the nature of desire and attachment. Love can appear as a conceptual object: definitions, causes, typologies, and debates about the relationship between love, reason, and the good life—often in an analytical tone rather than a lyrical one.

    POE — Poetic (shiʿrī). Poetry (especially ghazal and adjacent traditions) is the canonical space for passion, longing, separation, jealousy, and idealization, with dense figurative language. This genre tends to maximize emotional intensity and stylistic innovation, making it central for tracking lexical and imagistic changes in love discourse over time.

    RHE — Rhetorical / Adab (adabī). Adab and rhetorical literature includes anthologies, belles-lettres, anecdotes, moralized storytelling, and refined prose. Love appears as social performance and cultural literacy: witty scenes, normative advice, erotic insinuation, urbanity, and the management of desire through language and etiquette.

    THE — Theological / Scholastic (kalām). Kalām and theological texts discuss doctrine, orthodoxy, and the rational defense of beliefs. Love may surface in relation to God, belief, obedience, and ethical frameworks—less as romance, more as a theology of attachment.

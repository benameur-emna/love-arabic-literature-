Methods

المنهجية

How we turn love discourse into auditable signals (without killing the poetry)

Overview

Our goal is not to “measure love” as an emotion, but to estimate the semantic density of love-related discourse in each text. We do so using a Bag-of-Concepts (BoC) model: instead of counting isolated words (Bag-of-Words), we estimate how strongly a text aligns with conceptual registers of love (explicit love, desire, beauty, metaphor, spiritual love, etc.), using root-based lexical cues adapted to Arabic morphology.

Each text receives:

- nine register scores (BoC dimensions), each on a continuous scale 0–2, and

- a Love Index, a single composite score designed to approximate the overall “love intensity” of the text in a way that remains comparable across genres and centuries.

How it works

1 Bag-of-Concepts: love as a map, not a word count

A classic Bag-of-Words approach counts surface words. That’s fragile in Arabic: morphology is rich, spelling varies, and love is often coded (metaphor, imagery, etiquette).

So we use a Bag-of-Concepts (BoC) model:

  • BoW: “Does the word appear?”
  • BoC: “Does the text sound like this concept, across many lexical cues and roots?”
2 The love palette: our BoC registers

Each register is a curated set of Arabic roots + lexical cues associated with one “way of speaking love”. Scores are continuous (0–2): not yes/no, but how much.

BoC_anchorhigh precision

Explicit love. Direct markers (حب، محبة، عشق، غرام…). Catches passages that name love.

BoC_spirit

Devotional / mystical love. Longing as devotion: dhikr, purification, divine proximity.

BoC_human

Interpersonal attachment. Meeting, waiting, fidelity, jealousy, separation… love as bond.

BoC_desire

Sensual tension. Attraction, seduction, closeness — explicit or coded.

BoC_beauty

Grace / charm. Beauty, light, perfume, softness, bodies, faces, even scenery.

BoC_metaphor

Classical love imagery. Fire, burning, moon, night, wound, arrow, madness…

BoC_ethic

Love as virtue. Patience, loyalty, sincerity, mercy, moralized love language.

BoC_family

Beyond romance. Kinship, friendship, care, tenderness outside desire.

BoC_modern

Contemporary talk. Couple discourse, breakups, therapy, toxicity, social media…

3 What a score (0–2) actually means

A BoC score is a semantic density estimate:

  • 0 ≈ absent / negligible
  • ~1 ≈ meaningfully present
  • ~2 ≈ strongly shapes the text’s language

5 Why it works (the “ingredients”)

Rarity matters (IDF boost)

Rare love cues are more diagnostic; frequent generic words get down-weighted.

Idioms count

Fixed expressions carry meaning beyond isolated tokens; we apply a small boost.

Anchor proximity (coherence)

Beauty/fire/night can be non-love. If cues cluster near explicit anchors, the score rises.

Length normalization

Long books contain more of everything; we score density so texts are comparable.

Technical-language penalty

Conceptual definitions ≠ affective saturation; we prevent “definition-heavy” texts from dominating.

6 Love Index

The Love Index is not a direct sum of BoC scores. BoC registers are the starting point, but the final score also accounts for how Arabic love discourse works: rarity, idioms, contextual coherence, and fair comparison across texts.

What it measures. The density and coherence of love discourse in a text, not the author’s feelings, and not a simple keyword frequency.

What the index includes (in practice)

  • Register evidence (BoC): concept-level signals across roots and lexical cues (not a Bag-of-Words count).
  • Rarity (IDF boost): rare cues weigh more; very frequent terms carry less signal.
  • Idioms: strong collocations get a small boost (meaning beyond isolated tokens).
  • Anchor proximity: register cues contribute more when they cluster near explicit love anchors.
  • Length normalization: comparability across short and long texts.
  • Technical-language penalty: prevents “definition-heavy” texts from scoring as love-saturated.
  • Density: final values are expressed as a comparable score (e.g., per 1,000 tokens).

Transparent aggregation (readable baseline)

After these adjustments, we summarize registers using a weighted mean, to get the closest possible to the most common perception of 'Love' as a concept. We keep weights manual (for now): readable, debatable, testable. The weighted aggregation step is only one component of the index. Weights operate on already adjusted register scores and do not represent raw frequency counts.

7 What we store per text
  • the nine BoC scores (0–2)
  • the Love Index
  • diagnostic counters (anchors, proximity stats)
  • metadata (century, genre)

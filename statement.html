About the Project

بيان المشروع

Mapping love in Arabic literature

Arabic love is rarely plain

Arabic rarely gives love to you in a plain sentence. It prefers to let it seep in.

Love arrives as heat and fever, as a night that refuses to end, as the discipline of patience (ṣabr), the ache of separation (firāq), the mirage of union (waṣl). It appears in the ruins of an abandoned camp (aṭlāl), in the gaze that cannot settle, in a gazelle that never quite stops. Even when the lexicon is direct (ḥubb, ʿishq, maḥabba) it has been shaped by centuries of rhetoric, ethics, and theology. A word can be celebrated in a verse, questioned in a moral treatise, transformed into a spiritual concept in mystical writing.

This project begins with that asymmetry: what feels obvious inside the tradition can remain invisible from outside it. The aim is to make that visibility measurable, carefully, without mistaking measurement for meaning.

What we’re trying to show

Not whether people “loved more” or “loved less”, that question is too blunt. But whether love becomes more or less visible in writing, how it is allowed to appear, and how it moves across time and across literary traditions.

We work with a large-scale corpus (OpenITI/Shamela) to trace long-term shifts in love discourse: the language that names love, evokes it, frames it, disciplines it, spiritualizes it, or hides it in images.

The promise of the site is simple: to let readers move from patterns to texts, and from texts back to patterns, without forcing love into a single definition.

The questions that guide the project

  • Time. Does love discourse follow a long curve (peaks, declines, reappearances) across more than a millennium? If so, are we seeing a quantitative shift, or a change in what counts as a legitimate way to speak of love?
  • Tradition. Do different literary worlds carry love differently, or silence it differently?
  • Profane vs spiritual. Where does romantic longing end and devotional love begin? How often do the two borrow each other’s vocabulary?
  • Saying vs suggesting. When love is present, is it named directly, or built through recurring signs (metaphors, motifs, narrative situations) that function like a cultural shorthand?

What you can unlock on the site right now

  • A long-range view of love discourse over time. A first diachronic trajectory of overall love visibility in the corpus.
  • A way to explore texts individually. An interactive view where each point corresponds to a work, so you can connect the curve to specific titles, authors, genres, and dates.
  • A grounded sense of scale. Examples at both ends of the distribution (high vs low Love Index) to keep the analysis tethered to real texts.

What will unlock next

  • Filtering love by “type”. Exploring distinct love dimensions rather than a single overall score.
  • Profane vs spiritual, a moving boundary? A dedicated lens to distinguish interpersonal love from devotional/mystical love, and to track where they converge or diverge over time.
  • Saying vs suggesting. A clearer separation between love that is directly named and love that emerges through recurrent imagery, motifs, and coded discourse.
  • Traditions in sharper focus. Reader-facing comparisons between literary traditions.
  • A dynamic map (long-term ambition). If metadata allows, adding a spatio-temporal layer: seeing how love discourse moves not only through centuries, but across regions and cultural circuits.

A note on the project’s current state

This site presents preliminary findings. The corpus is large, the historical span is long, and the model is an instrument: it makes certain forms of love discourse visible, and it inevitably misses others.

So the results here are not a final verdict, they are a first readable surface: a way to test hypotheses, refine categories, and invite the reader into a research process that remains open, revisable, and accountable.
